The river sounded like stone meeting stone under a long argument that never picked a winner. She liked that about it. It meant the water was busy with its own work and would not bother to judge hers.

Her cousin had claimed the sunny patch first, knees in the dust, back turned like ownership. He was the kind of boy who always sat like he owned the ground he touched. “You’re going to snag it,” he said without looking up. He meant the cord her mother had measured against her arm three times that week, then coiled and hung out of reach on a peg too high for pride alone.

“I won’t,” she said.

“You will. You always want your hands in everything.”

That was unfair. She did not want her hands in *everything*. She wanted them in the places where the women went quiet when she came close, where the air smelled like river-cold and dye and sugar burned down to something serious.

Today the quiet was different—not empty, full. Women moved between the racks and the hearths with a rhythm that looked like play from far off and like work up close. Bundles appeared and disappeared. Hands passed knots along as if knots were gossip that needed to be told correctly.

She thought the binding year was about being measured so the world would finally admit she was tall enough.

Her mother caught her wrist once, not hard, the way you stop a jar before it tips. “Thumb here,” her mother murmured, repositioning. “Eyes on what your fingers do, not on what you wish they did.”

She wished her fingers could do what her grandmother’s did: make a line lie flat without arguing.

Her grandmother sat with three other women at a low frame, cloth taut as a skin. The old woman did not look up when children passed; she did not need to. The tilt of her head was a door—sometimes open, sometimes not. When it was open, you could step into it without speaking.

She stepped.

Her grandmother’s hand paused mid-thread, then tapped the edge of the frame once. Not permission, not refusal. A kind of punctuation.

“You’re underfoot,” her cousin hissed behind her.

“I’m not under anything,” she whispered back, which was both true and wrong, because the whole morning felt like a roof being built over the day.

One of the younger women laughed under her breath—not at her, not quite. “She’s learning how to carry a weight before she carries it,” the woman said to no one in particular.

That made pride prickle along her scalp. She straightened her shoulders the way she’d seen her mother straighten hers when visitors came up the path with their feet too eager.

Visitors meant trade cloth, sometimes sugar in a twist, sometimes a joke that wasn’t funny yet. Binding years meant something else—something the adults handled with their mouths closed and their eyes busy.

She had decided, privately, that binding meant the village would finally give her a real job instead of pretend ones.

Her mother had tried to explain once, voice low, words chosen like stones for a path. “It’s not a prize,” her mother said. “It’s a knot that tightens slowly.”

She had nodded because nodding made adults stop worrying at you. Inside, she had translated: *prize* was the wrong word anyway. What she wanted was *place*. A place where people stopped measuring her against her cousin’s longer legs and started measuring her against something that mattered.

Now she watched the measured cord swing slightly when someone brushed the peg. It moved like a fish on a line, alive without agreeing to be caught.

Her grandmother lifted her chin at last, eyes catching her—not a smile, not a scold. A weighing that did not feel cruel. The way you weigh a pot before you trust it with heat.

“Come here,” her grandmother said.

Her cousin sucked in a breath, jealous as a spark.

She came.

Her grandmother did not make room with speeches. She shifted a basket with her foot, slid a small bone awl aside, and set a short practice strip in front of her—not the good cloth, not the river-pale lengths the women guarded like eggs. Rougher fiber. Forgiving. For mistakes.

“Show me your knot,” her grandmother said.

She flushed. “I practiced.”

“Show me.”

Her hands knew the first loop and then forgot, because knowing and showing were different kinds of truth. The fiber tightened wrong. The loop turned stubborn.

Her grandmother waited three heartbeats—long enough for shame to arrive, not long enough for it to settle in and build a house.

Then two fingers, warm and dry, adjusted hers. Not taking the work away. Redirecting labor the way a current redirects a stick: without insult, without debate.

“Slow earns straight,” her grandmother murmured.

“I want to be fast,” she admitted, because the words slipped out before she could make them pretty.

Her grandmother’s mouth twitched—not quite a joke, not quite not. “Fast is how you learn what your hands refuse to learn.”

Someone farther down the line called for more thread. Someone else answered with a clatter like pottery kissing pottery. A baby fussed once and stopped, soothed by a motion she couldn’t see—hip, cloth, breath—someone’s rhythm.

Her cousin edged closer, pretending he only wanted shade.

“You’re still going to snag it,” he muttered.

She didn’t answer. She watched her grandmother’s thumb press the fiber into obedience without crushing it. She smelled smoke and river and the green bite of something crushed underfoot on the path.

When the strip finally held—ugly but honest—her grandmother nodded once. “Again,” she said. “Before you brag.”

She grinned, then tried to wipe the grin off because bragging was childish.

Too late. Her mother had seen. Her mother’s eyes softened in that small way adults pretended children didn’t notice.

Later—small, ordinary thing—her mother tied a scrap of clean cloth around the practice cord at the peg, a little flag of no importance, the kind of marker you’d use so you didn’t grab the wrong bundle in a hurry.

“So you know which coil is yours,” her mother said lightly.

She knew it wasn’t only that.

She didn’t ask.

She went to the bank where the mud was slick and the shells lay in broken half-moons, pale as old pottery. She picked one with a clean chip missing from its rim, slipped it into her pocket, and pressed her palm over it through the fabric until it warmed.

The river brightened and broke in the sun.

She practiced the knot again badly on purpose, just a little, because bad loops sometimes made her grandmother’s hands return faster than perfect ones.

Her grandmother did not come this time. The village held her elsewhere—voice low, directing, steering labor with touches and timing.

That stung, then didn’t.

She sat with the shell in her pocket and the cord’s shadow thin on her knee and watched the path where visitors would arrive if the day decided to widen.

Her cousin threw a pebble into the water. Ripples ran outward, small, serious, gone.

She let the measuring stay a mystery with a shape—cord, peg, her mother’s light marker on the coil—and did not force it into a sentence.

The coil marker fluttered once, hardly more than a breath. Her palm sweated against the shell inside her pocket.

She listened—hands, knots, laughter catching and breaking like light on scales—and waited for the next thing her hands were allowed to do.
