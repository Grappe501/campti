The mist was not white so much as pearl where it met the water, and the air tasted of mud and last night's smoke and something green trying to survive the tail of the cold. She stood calf-deep where the bank shelved gently, watching how the current stitched itself around her ankles. The river was always telling you a thing if you bothered to listen in the right language: not the language of men who wanted answers fast, but the language of patience, of silt rolling along the skin, of small fish making nervous silver notches against the deeper shadow.

"Aunt," a girl called from behind her—one of her daughter's children, voice still sticky with sleep. "Aunt, the kettle."

"Tell your mother I said the kettle can wait until the fire remembers how to behave."

The girl laughed because laughter was part of morning, and ran back toward the hearths where smoke already leaned in practiced directions.

Women had been up longer than laughter. You could hear it in the scrape of blades on wood, in the hollow knock of pottery being tested for hairline cracks before it held something hot, in the rhythmic slap of fish on a stone while scales lifted in tiny arcs that caught first light like flecks of mica. She had not needed to walk the whole village to know which households were nervous last night and which were merely busy; the sound of work changed when worry sat down at the door. She had learned that as a girl, standing here in the same shallow place, watching her own mother's shoulders soften or tighten before the rest of the day admitted what it was.

Now others watched her the way weather was watched—glances that touched and left, that waited to see if she would turn toward the eastern path where strangers sometimes came, or toward the drying racks where finfish bellied out silver and the blueblack of something brought from farther water gleamed in a way local fish did not.

She looked, deliberately, at the racks.

Two women already stood there, one older than her, one young enough to still grow into her wrists. They straightened when she came—not like soldiers, like kin who knew that some knowledge moved easier when it had a body to arrive in.

"The dark one," the younger murmured. "It came wrapped different."

Different was a careful word. It meant more than wrapping. It meant weight and smell and the way your hands felt after you touched something that had traveled through other hands.

"We eat what we know first," she said. "We treat what we don't know like a guest: feed it, keep it where it won't frighten the children, and watch it."

"We already put the copper aside," the older woman added, barely a confession.

Copper had its own smell when touched, thin and honest. It also had a habit of making people hurry.

"Good," she said. "Copper can wait for hands that aren't rushed."

That was not only about metal. The younger looked down, understanding without needing it drawn in lines.

Children threaded between the racks, chasing each other until an adult hiss turned the chase into something slower—a game that pretended to be about carrying kindling and was really about staying within earshot. One boy tried to lift a basket too big and succeeded only in tipping it; a girl his size caught the spill before it hit dust, a small satisfaction that made the morning brighter.

She watched the rescue with the part of her that recorded futures the way the river recorded debris: what would matter later, what could turn into skill, what would hurt if left alone.

"The shawl," the older woman said quietly, not quite a question.

The shawl had been folded three times and tied with fiber that wasn't local—tighter than their usual knots, impatient. Someone had thought ahead for another hand.

"When she is grown enough to carry a day without dropping it," she answered.

A thread of teasing rose in the younger's eyes, then receded into respect. Continuities lived in cloth as much as in blood. A binding was not only what you said in front of witnesses; it was how you began saving room for it in smoke and dye and the way you taught a girl to hold a needle so her fingers did not lie.

From the hearths came the day's first human roar—not anger, but the noise of many mouths and chores and dogs arguing over a bone that wasn't worth it. The river carried the noise back thinned, as if the water refused to keep more than it needed.

She returned to the bank and knelt, palms flat where the mud firmed. Cool climbed her wrists. When she closed her eyes the sun came through her lids in a dull rose, and for a moment she was a girl again, being corrected—

"Slower," her mother's voice had said, not unkindly. "The water is not your enemy. It is your relative that forgot your name."

She opened her eyes. A heron stepped, ridiculous and holy, along the far edge. Someone upstream coughed—one of the men waking late—and the sound was ordinary enough to be almost a comfort. Ordinary things were what you braided your strength from.

She rose and wiped her hands on her skirt once, not to clean them but to leave the river's claim where it belonged: on her skin, in her work, in the day.

"Aunt," the same girl called again, delighted now because repetition was also a kind of power. "Mother says the girl with the freckles wants you to look at her net."

"Then I will look," she said. "Tell your mother I am coming by the long way."

The long way meant she would pass three hearths, a line of drying herbs, and the place where visitors were supposed to wait if they did not know better. When she walked it, people did not stop her with speeches. They let their tasks speak: a lifted basket, a child redirected, a pot shifted from a flame that had grown eager overnight. They let their eyes ask, and her eyes answered in small units of yes, not yet, wait, I know.

By the time she reached the eastern bend where nets hung like shadows that had learned to hold silver, the sun had burned a hole clean through the mist. The river widened in the eye—more water than one life could drink, less water than tomorrow would need.

She touched the net the girl had worried over and found nothing wrong except fear.

"Here," she said, and showed how the knot could breathe.

The girl breathed with it.

That was the village, in miniature: knots that could breathe, copper set aside, fish unknown and therefore treated like guests, children learning rescue before rescue became necessary, cloth saved for a day still soft in the future.

And under all of it, water moving on without applause—always first, always last, carrying whatever the world set on it, listening in a tongue only patience spoke.
