The mist was pearl where it met the water, not a clean white. Mud and last night’s smoke sat in the air with the cold’s last teeth, and under that a green smell from the bank where new shoots pushed through rotted cane. She stood calf-deep on the shelving sand, feeling the current tug at her ankles the way a child tugs when it wants attention without words.

“Aunt,” a girl called behind her—one of her daughter’s children, voice still sticky with sleep. “Aunt, the kettle.”

“Tell your mother the kettle can wait until the fire remembers how to behave.”

The girl laughed and ran. Smoke over the hearths leaned the way it always leaned when women had already decided the day’s order.

Her daughter met the child halfway, arms full of willow strips, hair pushed back with the heel of a wet hand. “You’ll wear a groove in the path,” her daughter said to the girl, not scolding, correcting—the way you correct a habit you love.

The girl slowed for three steps, then sped again.

Her daughter came close enough to speak lower. “The dark fish still drips.”

“Then racks first,” she answered. “Smoke when the racks stop dripping, not when fear drips.”

Her daughter pressed her lips together, wanting speed, wanting the unfamiliar work finished before it could become a story. Then she nodded, because this was how you learned a mother’s tempo without agreeing yet in your bones.

Women had been up longer than laughter. The scrape of blades on wood. Pottery tapped for cracks before it took heat. Fish slapped on stone and scales lifted in arcs that caught light like mica. She did not need to walk the whole village to know where worry had slept last night; the sound of work had edges, like a basket with a broken rim.

Others watched her the way people watched weather—quick, then away—waiting to see whether she would turn toward the eastern path or toward the drying racks where silver bellies caught the thinning mist and one darker fish gleamed wrong in its skin, too blueblack for this bend.

She went to the racks because going first is also a kind of answer.

Two women waited there, one older than she was, one young enough that her wrists still looked surprised by their own strength. They straightened—not like soldiers. Like kin who had been holding a decision in their hands until hands could hand it to someone.

“The dark one,” the younger murmured. “Wrapped different.”

Different meant weight, smell, the slick feel of another river still clinging to scales.

“We eat what we know first,” she said. “What we don’t know eats outside the children’s running line. If it needs watching, someone watches in shifts. No one runs the racks alone.”

“I’ll take first watch,” her daughter said, too quickly.

“No,” she said. “You take the racks level. Let your hands learn patience where your mouth wants hurry.”

Her daughter’s jaw tightened—then released. She moved to the rack pins, testing the tilt with a practiced thumb, and the younger woman beside her softened too, as if the argument had been lifted off them and hung where it belonged: on wood, on work, not on faces.

“We put the copper aside,” the older woman admitted.

Copper smelled thin when touched, honest in the wrong way. It made people’s feet want shortcuts.

“Good,” she said. “Copper waits. Hands don’t rush copper.”

Not only copper.

Children threaded the racks until a hiss turned chase into kindling duty—still chase, still play, still within earshot. A boy tipped a basket; a girl caught the spill before dust could claim it. She watched and pointed with her chin toward the humming girl, who always knotted nets the way some people chewed thinking.

“Higher stack,” she said. “Before the dogs get interested.”

The humming girl obeyed before she understood, which was also training.

“The shawl,” the older woman murmured.

Folded three times. Fiber not from here, tied in a tightness that looked like someone else’s impatience.

“When she can carry a day without dropping it,” she said.

Her daughter took the needle from between her teeth—she had been holding it like a small bone without noticing—and set it in the basket where cloth waited. No speech about witnesses. No speech about forever. The shawl sat there like a door left ajar.

From the hearths came the day’s first crowded noise—many mouths, many chores, a dog’s jealous yelp over a bone not worth fighting for. The river took that noise and thinned it into itself until it became only another texture of moving water.

She walked back to the bank, knelt, pressed palms into mud where the sand firmed. Cool climbed her wrists. For a blink she was small again, her own mother behind her—

“Slower.”

Not unkind.

She opened her eyes. A heron picked along the far edge like a thing too serious for its legs. Upstream, a man coughed—late waking, ordinary—and nearer the eastern path a traveler’s bundle leaned against the post where people were supposed to leave hurry outside. The traveler sat on his heels in the correct dust, eyes on his hands, not on her. Good enough for now.

She rose, wiped her hands once on her skirt, not to clean them but to carry the river’s claim into the rest of the morning.

“Aunt,” the first girl called again, pleased with repetition. “Mother says the humming girl needs your eyes on her net.”

“Then I’ll come the long way,” she said.

The long way passed three hearths, a line of herbs drying string-taut, the visitor’s post. She did not stop for speeches. A lifted basket waited until she passed. A child was lifted out of a dog’s path. A pot was nudged from a flame that had grown eager overnight. Her daughter fell in beside her for two hearths, not speaking, matching steps—another kind of listening.

At the eastern bend the nets hung like shadows veined with silver. Mist had torn open; sun heated cypress bark until the smell of it rose sweet and rough.

The humming girl held out the net as if offering a heart.

She found nothing wrong except fear.

“Here,” she said, and showed how the knot could breathe.

The girl breathed with it.

A loosened scale caught on her wet thumb. Before she could think what to do with it, the river took it—gone—small as a lie forgiven early.

She stayed another moment with her hand on the cord, feeling the water’s pull through the fibers, and let the morning keep its name without her naming it.
