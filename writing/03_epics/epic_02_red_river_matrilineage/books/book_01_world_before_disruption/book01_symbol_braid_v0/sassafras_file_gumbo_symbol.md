# Sassafras / Filé / Gumbo Symbol — Book 01

Canon status: PROPOSED pending research

## Purpose

Introduce ground sassafras as a symbol of Native knowledge, healing, food, continuity, and memory before later gumbo traditions emerge.

## Human direction

Ground sassafras should become a symbol stretching across centuries.

The same family knowledge of gathering sassafras leaves continues into the present: the tree where the human’s grandfather and great-grandfather picked the best sassafras leaves is still being picked today to make filé for gumbo.

This should become one of the epic’s living symbols.

## Book 01 function

In Book 01, sassafras should not yet be “modern gumbo.”

It should appear as:

- plant knowledge
- women’s knowledge
- healing soup / thickening / flavoring research path
- child learning from elders
- memory tied to place
- leaf gathering
- seasonal knowledge
- food as medicine
- household continuity
- Native world detail

## Research anchors to verify

- Native use of sassafras leaves
- Choctaw filé / ground leaf tradition
- medicinal uses of sassafras root bark, leaves, and poultices
- whether Chitimacha / Caddo / Natchitoches contexts have specific sassafras uses
- whether healing soups can be supported for this specific community
- difference between leaves, root bark, and safrole safety concerns
- seasonality of leaf gathering
- drying / grinding methods
- whether the word filé belongs later through French naming

## Symbolic meaning

| Layer | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Sassafras tree | living continuity across centuries |
| Leaves | knowledge gathered by hand |
| Ground filé | memory made usable |
| Soup / stew | healing, feeding, binding the household |
| Gumbo later | blended history of Native, French, African, and family survival |
| Family tree still picked today | proof that the line remembers through practice |

## Book 01 use

Possible uses:

- elder woman teaching a child which leaves to choose
- mother or grandmother adding ground leaves to a healing broth or thick soup if historically supported
- child associating the smell with safety
- sassafras tied to women’s knowledge and place
- leaf-gathering as inheritance
- a small pouch or bundle of dried leaves as a memory object

## What not to do

- Do not call it modern gumbo too early.
- Do not force French roux into Book 01 unless historically justified.
- Do not force African okra influence into Book 01 before the timeline supports it.
- Do not claim a specific tribal use without evidence.
- Do not over-symbolize the object so it stops feeling useful.
