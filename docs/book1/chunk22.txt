If **April 9, 1682** is your opening chapter title, then the power of it is not that LaSalle appears on page one. For your book, the Spanish story absolutely needs to be present from the beginning as a **pressure in the background**, then gradually come into focus as the frontier tightens. In your region, Spain was not far away or irrelevant. **Los Adaes**, in present-day Louisiana near modern Robeline, began as a Spanish mission effort in 1716 and was reestablished in 1721 as a mission-presidio complex specifically to counter the nearby French presence at Natchitoches. That means in story terms:

A strong way to build your timeline is this:

**1542** — first recorded Spanish description of Caddo-region peoples comes from the **Moscoso phase of the De Soto expedition**, after De Soto’s death. ### On sickness and Native understanding

Your instinct here is strong. Native communities would not have “understood” epidemic disease in European biological terms. They would have understood that after certain contacts came catastrophic death. Some scholarship on East Texas Caddo notes repeated epidemic outbreaks during the colonial era and severe population decline, especially in communities with closer colonial contact. ([ResearchGate][8])

So in the opening Native-centered book, you can portray disease as:

* an unexplained rupture in the sacred order
* a terror tied to outsiders but not fully legible
* something interpreted through spiritual, communal, and relational frameworks rather than germ theory
* a force that changes diplomacy, mourning, kinship, and fear

That lets you avoid anachronism while making the impact devastatingly real.

### On the census question

Yes, you should absolutely chase Spanish census material.

The best lead I found is that **Spanish and French Louisiana kept local and military censuses from 1699 to 1805**, with originals in the **Archivo General de Indias** in Seville. There are also published guides and compilations specifically pointing to **Natchitoches colonial censuses, military rolls, and tax lists, 1722–1803**. ([FamilySearch][14])

The most useful archival leads for you are:

* **Archivo General de Indias (AGI)** via the **PARES** portal for digitized Spanish records and descriptions of Louisiana materials. 
* **Louisiana State Archives subject guide**, which points to **Natchitoches Parish civil records** including conveyances and miscellaneous colonial material from the 18th century. 
* Published reference works like **Elizabeth Shown Mills, *Natchitoches Colonials: Censuses, Military Rolls, and Tax Lists, 1722–1803***, which looks especially relevant for your family and place research. 

So the answer is: yes, Spanish census material exists or can be reconstructed through census substitutes, but it may live partly in published abstracts and partly in Spanish archive systems rather than one neat local collection.

## Character Truth Profile: Jacques Guédon

### What looks strong

Jacques Guédon appears in the literature as a **settler from Nantes** who became part of the early Natchitoches frontier and married a **Chitimacha woman, Thérèse de la Grande Terre**. Multiple historical and genealogical works repeat that basic pairing, and the Natchitoches/Los Adaes border context fits it well. One careful evidence-review piece specifically quotes the claim that Guédon was “a settler from Nantes,” while also showing how later authors layered extra assumptions onto that core fact. ([evidenceexplained.com][1])

The broader setting is right, too: Los Adaes was the Spanish presidio-mission complex built in response to French intrusion, became the capital of Spanish Texas in 1729, and sat on El Camino Real, the political and commercial corridor linking Mexico City and Los Adaes. That makes a Guédon family story spanning **Natchitoches, Los Adaes, Native kinship, and contraband trade** historically plausible. ([Louisiana State Parks][2])

There is also credible support for the larger pattern that **French men at Natchitoches married Native women from southern Louisiana**, including a Guédon–de la Grande Terre union. A TCU dissertation notes that François Derbanne and Jacques Guédon, both associated with Natchitoches, married Chitimacha women from farther south rather than local Red River tribes. ([TCU Digital Repository][3])

### What looks plausible but not yet fully locked
