## Best opening year

I would open the novel in **1653**.

That year works cleanly for the family timeline you want:

* **First matriarch** born **1637**, enters womanhood and marriage around **1653** at about 16.
* **Second matriarch** born **1656**, and make her the **middle child** of the first matriarch.
* **Third matriarch** born **1675**, exactly as you want.
* If the third matriarch has her first daughter in **1690**, that daughter is **9–11 years old in 1699–1701**, which fits your later first-contact memory beat very naturally.

That gives you a Book 1 span of roughly **1653 to 1699/1700**, which is long enough to fully develop three generations without making the timeline feel strained.

## Why 1653 works historically

By the **late 1600s**, Caddo populations had already been reduced by epidemic disease spreading through Indigenous trade networks, even before full colonial occupation of the region. The National Park Service says Caddo populations had fallen dramatically by the 1680s and that by **1686** the Caddo lived primarily in small groups on the Red River and in East Texas. ([National Park Service][1])

At the same time, the French outpost at Natchitoches was not founded until **1714**, and the Spanish mission/presidio complex at Los Adaes belongs to the **1710s–1720s**, not the 1650s. ([texasbeyondhistory.net][2])

So **1653** gives you a world that is already historically real and fully Native in political control, while still leaving room for distant Spanish traces, rumors, old epidemic memory, and subtle long-range disturbance.

## The key historical caution

There is **no defensible archival exact census** for the Campti/Natchitoches region in **1653**. The published estimates are too scattered and often conflict with each other. For example, one NPS synthesis says the Caddo population had dropped to about **10,000 by the 1680s**, while TSHA material cited for the Hasinai alone places them at roughly **10,000 around 1690**. ([National Park Service][1])

So the right move is:
