A woman did not come empty-handed. She did not arrive as a body to be fitted into another house. She arrived carrying labor, memory, and continuity. What she brought was not payment. It was proof that she understood what joining meant.

The older woman nodded once toward the basket.

“You came with food.”

It was not praise. It was recognition.

The young woman bowed her head slightly. “My mother said a house should know your hands before it knows your sleeping.”

At that, the second woman gave the smallest sound through her nose—not laughter, exactly, but something close enough to soften the room.

“Your mother speaks like a woman who has carried many winters,” she said.

“She has,” the young woman replied.

Silence followed. Not strained. Measured.

The older woman gestured toward the fire. “Come closer.”

The young woman moved from the doorway to the hearth and sat where the warmth could reach her legs. She folded her hands loosely in her lap, though she had to resist the urge to keep them busy. To touch the edge of the basket. To smooth the skin beside her. To prove herself through movement.

Across from her, the older woman leaned forward and added a small split of wood to the fire. Sparks jumped, rose, disappeared.

“This fire was not lit for you,” she said.

The words were plain, but not cruel.

The young woman lifted her eyes. “I know.”

The older woman watched her a moment longer, then looked back to the flames.

“It was lit by my mother’s hands long before mine. And before hers, by another woman whose name I never heard, because by the time I was old enough to ask, no one still living had known her.”

She shifted the wood slightly with a stick, settling it deeper into the coals.

“A house does not begin when a man chooses a place to sleep.”

The other woman snorted softly at that, still sorting her beans.

“It begins,” the elder continued, “when a fire is kept long enough that the smoke knows the roof.”

The young woman let the words settle.

This, she understood, was the true beginning. Not the gathering earlier. Not the exchange between families. Not the walk from one cluster of houses to another under the eyes of kin. This. Sitting before the fire that would now measure her. Listening to the women who held its memory.

The second woman set the basket aside and reached for a clay bowl near the hearth. She held it out.

“Stir.”

The young woman took it with both hands. Inside, a thick mixture of meal and water was beginning to gather heat at the edge, not yet fully cooking. She found the stirring stick where it had been laid and began to move it slowly, scraping the bottom so it would not catch.

No one thanked her.

That, too, was part of it.

The older woman leaned back slightly.

“You know how to keep a fire?”

“Yes.”

“You know how to clean a fish without wasting the flesh?”

“Yes.”

“You know when corn is ready to cut and when it only looks ready?”

“Yes.”

“Can you tell from a child’s cough whether it needs steam, bitter root, or waiting?”

The young woman paused only a fraction before answering. “Sometimes.”

That drew both women’s eyes toward her.

She kept stirring.

The older woman’s mouth moved, almost imperceptibly.

“Good,” she said. “A woman who says yes to everything is one who has not yet had responsibility.”

The second woman nodded once, as though the answer had settled something important.

Outside, footsteps passed near the house and continued on. Voices rose and faded. Somewhere farther off, dogs barked once, then were quiet.

The older woman reached beside her and lifted a small piece of carved wood from the floor. It was worn from years of use, darkened by smoke and the oil of hands. A spoon. Nothing more. Nothing less.

She held it out.

“My mother used this until her fingers bent and could not close around it right,” she said. “Then I used it. Tonight you will use it.”

The young woman accepted it carefully.

The wood was warm.

She looked down at it, tracing with her thumb the shallow groove worn into the handle by another woman’s hand, and another before that. She imagined fingers pressing in the same place over years so long they had become shape.

Not gift.

Not decoration.

Inheritance.

The older woman saw understanding move across her face and did not interrupt it.

“When a woman enters a house,” she said, “she does not erase the women who came before. She makes a place large enough to hold them and still keep breathing.”

The young woman lowered her gaze to the fire again.

The meal thickened under her hand. She adjusted the bowl’s position slightly, letting it catch less heat on one side. Small things. Necessary things. The kind that made a house run without anyone speaking of them.

The second woman rose and crossed the room, lifting a bundle from a peg in the wall. She laid it beside the young woman without comment.

A folded skin. A small woven mat. Space made visible.

The young woman looked at it, then up.

The woman shrugged. “You will sleep here.”

There was no ceremony in it. No moment marked as sacred. But the simplicity of it struck her harder than any words had.

You will sleep here.

Not tonight only.

Here.

The fire popped. A coal split and fell inward.

The older woman reached for the basket the young woman had brought and untied it. She examined the contents slowly: meal, dried leaves, a small pouch of salt traded from farther west, two strings of beans, and at the bottom, wrapped carefully in soft bark, three beads the color of deep water after rain.

She lifted the beads and held them so the firelight moved across them.

“From your mother?”

The young woman nodded.

The elder handed them back without asking their history. That mattered too. A woman’s first gifts from her mother into a new house were not for questioning.

“Keep them,” she said.

The young woman tucked them into the fold of her dress.

Outside, the light was lowering fast now. Evening had settled fully over the village. The house seemed to draw inward with it, becoming its own world of smoke, heat, and breath.

The second woman returned to her mat and began working again, this time with a length of cord that needed tightening. The older woman reached for another stick of wood and laid it beside the fire for later. The young woman continued to stir until the meal was ready, then shifted the bowl aside just as her own mother would have done.

No one told her she had done it well.

No one needed to.

The elder watched her a long while in silence, then spoke without looking up.

“A house sees everything.”

The young woman waited.

“It sees how you wake. How you carry water. Whether you leave ashes scattered. Whether you hear a child cry before it cries twice. Whether you speak when silence would do more good. Whether you eat the best portion or pass it on.”

The woman finally lifted her eyes.

“A house knows before people do.”

The young woman felt those words more than heard them.

Across the hearth, smoke moved upward and disappeared into the dark curve of the roof.

She looked at the flame. At the spoon in her hand. At the skin folded near where she would sleep.

Then she looked at the elder and gave the only answer that would matter.

“I will listen.”

The elder held her gaze.

Then, for the first time since the young woman entered, she gave a true nod—small, but unmistakable.

“Good,” she said.

Nothing more.

The second woman rose to portion the meal. The elder reached for bowls. The young woman moved when needed and not before. Hand to bowl. Bowl to mat. Fire to food. Food to hands.

The work flowed without instruction.

When they finally ate, the room had gone fully dark except for the fire. Outside, the village had quieted into the sounds of night—frogs in the low places, insects rising, an occasional voice from another house blurred by distance and smoke.

No one said a blessing.

None was needed.

The blessing was the fire. The food. The women. The fact of the house still standing with breath inside it.

Later, when the bowls were cleaned and the coals settled low, the older woman lay down on her mat without farewell. The second woman wrapped herself in skin and turned to the wall. One by one, the sounds of the house changed from movement to rest.

The young woman remained seated a moment longer before the hearth.

She had not cried. She had not wanted to. There was nothing in her that felt broken. But there was a quiet emptiness where one life had ended and another had not yet fully taken hold.

She laid the spoon carefully beside the hearth where the elder had first kept it.

Then she reached toward the coals with the fire stick and drew them inward, closer together, making sure the heat would hold until morning.

It was a small act.

No one saw her do it.

But as she lowered herself onto her mat for the first time in that house, she understood something her mother had never needed to explain.

A woman entered a house in one day.

But a house admitted her slowly.

And somewhere, beyond sleep, beyond the breathing of the women around her, beyond even the reach of the firelight, the river continued moving through the dark—steady, unseen, remembering.





The river was wider here.

Not deeper—she could still read its movement by the way the surface pulled and released—but wider, stretched between banks that no longer mirrored each other. On one side, cypress and willow leaned close, their roots drinking steadily from the slow edge. On the other, the land rose back sooner, giving way to open ground before the trees gathered again.

She sat in the front of the canoe.

Not because she had been told to. Because that was where she had been placed.

Behind her, her husband moved the paddle in long, even strokes. He did not look at her. He did not need to. The rhythm between them had already settled into something understood—his movement, her stillness, the river carrying both.

Two other canoes moved with them.

One ahead, one slightly behind.

No one spoke.

The only sounds were the quiet cut of wood against water and the occasional shift of weight as someone adjusted their position. Even the birds seemed farther away here, their calls softened by distance and the wide, open stretch of river.

In her lap rested a bundle.

Wrapped in hide, tied carefully, balanced so that it would not shift if the canoe rocked. Inside were things that had been gathered, made, or traded across many hands:

* dried meat, prepared to last
* ground corn sealed tight against damp
* a small pouch of salt that had come from far west, carried longer than she had yet traveled
* beads, smoothed by time and exchange
* two carved tools her husband’s family had shaped themselves

Nothing inside belonged only to her.

Everything inside would pass on.

She rested her hands lightly over the bundle, not holding it, but aware of it.

Ahead, the lead canoe slowed.

The man in the back lifted his paddle slightly and angled it, guiding them toward the right bank where the land rose more quickly. No signal was called. No instruction given.

They all knew.

She felt the shift in the water before she saw it—the subtle pull that meant the current narrowed, that the river was giving way to a place where people met it more directly.

The bank came into clearer view.

Not empty.

Never empty.

There were signs, if you knew how to look:

* a cut in the reeds where canoes had been drawn up
* a darker patch of earth where feet had pressed repeatedly
* a broken branch at shoulder height, not fallen but moved

They were expected.

Or at least, they were not unexpected.

The lead canoe reached the bank first. The man in front stepped out into the shallow water, pulling the canoe up just enough that it would not drift. The others followed, one by one, each movement controlled, deliberate, leaving nothing to chance.

She stepped last.

The mud took her foot, then released it as she shifted her weight forward onto firmer ground. She did not look back at the canoe. Her attention moved ahead, to the line where the trees began again.

They did not enter immediately.

They waited.

Not long. Just enough.

Then, from within the trees, movement.

Three figures emerged.

Not quickly. Not cautiously. Simply… present.

Two men. One woman.

Different.

Not in a way that would alarm. Not in a way that would confuse. But different enough that the small details marked them clearly to anyone who had learned to see:

* the cut of their hair
* the way their clothing sat against their bodies
* the pattern of beads at the throat, not quite like those worn in her own village
* the spacing between them as they walked

Yatasi.

She did not think the name. She felt it.

The woman stepped forward first.

Her eyes moved across the group—not searching, not judging, but taking in what was there and placing it where it belonged. When her gaze reached the young woman, it paused—not long enough to be rude, but long enough to register.

Then it moved on.

The men exchanged brief acknowledgments with her husband and the others. No greetings were spoken aloud. None were needed. The meeting itself was the greeting.

They moved toward a space slightly back from the river, where the ground leveled and a fallen log marked a natural boundary. This was where it would happen.

The bundles were brought forward.

Not opened yet.

Placed between them.

The woman from the other group crouched first, settling easily onto her heels. The others followed, forming a loose circle—not closed, not fixed, but balanced.

The young woman remained slightly behind her husband.

Not hidden.

Not central.

Watching.

The first bundle was untied.

Inside: dried fish, smaller than those taken from the deeper bends of the river near her home, but plentiful and well-prepared. The smell was clean, strong, unmistakable.

Her husband nodded once.

Another bundle opened.

Beans. Different from those grown near her village. Slightly darker. Slightly smaller. But good.

Hands moved.

Items were lifted, turned, examined.

No one rushed.

Time did not press here.

She watched the woman from the other group closely.

The way she touched each item told more than any words could:

* the slight pause over the salt—valued
* the quick, efficient check of the tools—understood
* the longer consideration of the beads—measured

Nothing was hidden.

Nothing was exaggerated.

This was not a place for deception.

It was a place for balance.

One of the men spoke then—low, measured.

A statement, not a request.

Her husband responded.

Not immediately.

He let the words settle, then answered in the same tone, matching weight for weight, neither giving more nor less than what had been offered.

The young woman felt the exchange more than followed it.

It was not the words that mattered.

It was:

* when they were spoken
* how long they waited between them
* where the eyes rested
* where they did not

A slight shift.

The woman across from her reached into her own bundle and drew out something wrapped separately.

She unwrapped it slowly.

Inside was a string of beads unlike the others.

Not brighter.

Not larger.

But shaped differently—subtle variations that spoke of a place farther than this meeting point, farther even than the places her husband had named on their journey.

The woman held them up briefly so the light could move across them.

Then she extended her hand.

Not to the men.

To her.

The young woman did not move at first.

She felt her husband’s attention shift beside her—not toward her, but aware of her.

The moment held.

Then she stepped forward.

Not too quickly.

Not hesitating.

She knelt and accepted the beads with both hands.

The woman’s fingers brushed hers briefly.

A contact so light it could have been missed.

But it was not.

In that touch was recognition.

Not of who she was.

But of what she was becoming.

The young woman lowered her eyes slightly, acknowledging the exchange without claiming it.

She did not look back as she returned to her place.

The beads rested in her hands, heavier than their size should allow.

The trade continued.

Bundles shifted.

Agreements formed—not spoken as decisions, but understood as they settled into place.

When it was done, nothing marked the ending.

No signal.

No closing word.

Only a gradual stilling of movement.

The bundles, now different from before, were tied again.

The circle loosened.

People stood.

The woman from the other group met her eyes once more.

This time, the pause was shorter.

But it remained.

Then she turned and walked back toward the trees.

The others followed.

Within moments, they were gone.

No lingering.

No watching from a distance.

Trust meant leaving.

The young woman stood still for a moment longer.

The river moved behind her, steady, unchanged.

In her hands, the new beads pressed against her skin.

She turned them slightly, feeling their shape, their unfamiliar balance.

Something in her shifted.

Not large.

Not yet.

But present.

She had seen it now.

Not the trade.

Not the goods.

But the space between people where everything was decided.

And she understood—without needing to say it—that she would stand in that space again.

Many times.

Behind her, her husband lifted the bundle and nodded once toward the canoe.

They would move on.

There were other places.

Other people.

Other exchanges waiting to be made.

She stepped back toward the river.

This time, she did not take the rear position.

She did not think about it.

She simply moved.

And when she settled into the canoe again, the bundle in her lap felt different than it had before.

Not because of what it held.

But because of what she now understood it to carry.

The canoe pushed off.

The bank slipped away.

The river took them again—steady, patient, remembering.




The voices carried before the people came into view.

Not loud. Not shouting. But tight.

The kind of voices that did not rise because they did not need to. The kind that pressed instead—low, controlled, edged with something that had not yet decided whether it would become anger.

She heard them before she saw them.

She slowed.

The path narrowed slightly ahead, curving around a stand of low trees where the ground dipped before rising again toward a clearing. She knew the place. Everyone who moved between the river and the higher ground knew it. It was not a meeting place by design, but it had become one by use.

People stopped there when something needed to be said.

Or when something had already been said and could not be taken back.

She did not step into the clearing immediately.

She shifted slightly to the side of the path, where the brush gave just enough cover to see without being seen. Not hiding. Not intruding.

Waiting.

There were six of them.

Three from her village. Three from another—Doustioni, by the look of their dress, their spacing, the way they stood slightly angled toward one another rather than outward.

A bundle lay on the ground between them.

Untouched.

That was the first sign.

Trade goods did not sit untouched unless something had gone wrong.

One of the men from her village spoke again.

“It was counted before it left.”

His tone was even, but the words were set hard.

The man across from him shook his head once.

“It was not as you say when it reached us.”

Not louder.

Not defensive.

Just firm.

Another man shifted his weight, stepping half a pace forward before catching himself and settling again. The movement did not go unnoticed. It tightened something in the air, subtle but real.

The woman among the Doustioni group spoke then.

“You sent three lengths.”

“We sent four.”

A pause.

“We received three.”

The space between those statements held more weight than the words themselves.

No one reached for the bundle.

No one opened it again.

Because the truth of it was no longer in what lay inside.

It was in what had already been decided in each mind.

She stepped forward then.

Not quickly.

Not announcing herself.

Simply moving into the edge of the clearing until her presence became part of the space.

The voices stopped.

Not because she demanded it.

Because she had entered.

All eyes turned toward her—not sharply, not with surprise, but with the quiet shift of attention that happens when someone known for seeing clearly steps into something that has become clouded.

She did not greet them.

She did not ask what had happened.

She already knew enough.

She walked to the bundle and knelt beside it.

For a moment, she did nothing.

Her hands rested on her thighs. Her eyes moved over the wrapping—not opening it, not touching it, just seeing it as it was.

Then she looked up.

Not at the man from her village.

Not at the man from the Doustioni.

At the woman.

“You received three,” she said.

The woman held her gaze.

“Yes.”

“And you sent four,” she continued, turning her eyes now to the man from her side.

“Yes.”

The words were not challenged.

Not yet.

She nodded once, as if both statements could exist together without breaking anything.

Then she reached forward and untied the bundle.

Slowly.

Carefully.

Not because she needed to check.

Because the act of opening mattered.

Inside were the goods.

She lifted them one by one, placing them on the ground between them:

* a length of dried meat
* a small pouch of grain
* a wrapped set of tools
* another length of meat, smaller than the first

Four.

She did not say it.

She let them see.

The men shifted slightly.

Not in triumph.

Not in accusation.

In recognition that the count, at least here, was clear.

She looked again at the woman.

“You received three,” she repeated.

The woman’s jaw tightened, just slightly.

“Yes.”

The space between them held.

The others watched.

Waiting for the moment when one side would be declared right.

Waiting for the moment when the other would be made wrong.

It did not come.

Instead, she picked up the smaller length of meat and held it lightly in her hand.

“When you opened it,” she said, “was this here?”

The woman hesitated.

Not long.

Long enough.

“No.”

The word landed differently.

Not a denial.

A memory.

She turned her eyes to the man from her village.

“When you wrapped it, was this here?”

“Yes.”

This time, the answer came quickly.

Too quickly.

She did not challenge him.

She did not need to.

She lowered the piece back to the ground.

Then she sat back on her heels and let silence move through the group.

Not empty silence.

Working silence.

The kind that allowed something to surface without being forced.

Finally, she spoke.

“The river does not lose things,” she said.

No one responded.

“It carries them.”

A slight shift of wind moved through the clearing, brushing leaves together softly overhead.

She looked at the man from her village.

“Who carried this bundle?”

He gestured toward the younger man beside him.

The younger man stepped forward slightly.

“I did.”

She turned to him.

“Did you stop?”

He hesitated.

“Yes.”

“Where?”

“At the bend, before the crossing.”

“Alone?”

Another pause.

“Yes.”

The silence deepened.

She did not press further.

She did not ask what he had done there.

She did not need to.

She turned back to the group.

“The river carries things,” she said again. “But people carry them farther.”

The words settled.

Not accusing.

Not excusing.

Placing.

She reached for the goods again and began to rewrap them.

Four pieces.

As they had been sent.

As they had been received.

As they now would be again.

When the bundle was tied, she pushed it gently toward the Doustioni group.

“It will go again,” she said.

No one spoke.

The man from her village looked as if he might.

Then didn’t.

The woman from the Doustioni side studied the bundle, then lifted her eyes.

“And if it arrives as three again?”

This time, there was something beneath the words.

Not challenge.

Testing.

The first matriarch met her gaze steadily.

“Then it will be three,” she said.

The answer held no contradiction.

No attempt to force the outcome.

Only acceptance of what would be.

Something in the woman’s face shifted.

A tension eased.

Not fully.

Enough.

She nodded once.

The men followed her lead.

The moment that had threatened to become something else—something harder, something that would ripple outward beyond this clearing—loosened.

Not erased.

Redirected.

The bundle was lifted.

The path forward remained open.

The younger man did not meet her eyes.

She did not call him to.

She did not name what had happened.

Because naming it would make it something that had to be answered.

And what needed to happen here was not answer.

It was continuation.

She rose.

The others stepped back slightly to give her space, though no one had asked them to.

She moved past them toward the far side of the clearing, where the path rejoined the line that led back toward the river.

No one stopped her.

No one thanked her.

None of that belonged to this moment.

As she reached the edge of the trees, she heard the first voices begin again behind her.

Quieter now.

Less tight.

Work returning to where conflict had been.

She did not look back.

She stepped onto the path and continued forward, the sound of the river already beginning to rise again ahead.

Behind her, the clearing remained what it had always been:

A place where things could break.

And, if handled correctly,

a place where they did not have to.




The first child came in the late heat.

The air had held too long that day, thick and unmoving, pressing against the skin in a way that made even breath feel like work. The river had slowed under it, its surface flattening into long stretches of quiet where the current moved beneath without showing itself.

Inside the house, the fire had been lowered.

Not extinguished.

Never that.

But drawn down to coals so that heat would not rise too sharply into the space where it was no longer welcome.

She knelt on a woven mat, her hands braced against the packed earth beneath her, her breath coming steady but deeper than before. Around her, the women moved without urgency, but without pause.

No one asked if she was ready.

No one told her what would come.

The work had already begun.

Her husband was not inside.

He had not been sent away.

He had simply stepped out.

This was not his place.

Not because he lacked strength.

Because this strength belonged elsewhere.

The older woman—his mother—sat behind her, one hand resting firm against her lower back. Not pushing. Not guiding. Just there. A steady presence that did not waver.

Another woman—her mother’s sister—moved between fire and mat, adjusting what needed adjusting, watching the rhythm of her body as if reading something written just beneath the skin.

“You are holding too much,” the older woman said quietly.

The words were not sharp.

They were precise.

She exhaled slowly, letting her shoulders drop, letting something inside her release just enough to change the shape of the moment.

Outside, the village moved as it always did.

Children ran between houses. Men returned from the river with fish still glistening. Voices rose and fell, unbroken by what was happening inside.

Life did not stop for birth.

Birth moved inside life.

Another wave came.

She leaned forward into it, her hands pressing harder into the earth. The older woman’s hand shifted slightly, adjusting with her, matching her without taking control.

“Let it move,” her aunt said from the fire.

“I am,” she answered, though the word came thinner than she intended.

“No,” the aunt replied, not unkindly. “You are holding it.”

The difference mattered.

She closed her eyes.

The river came to her then—not as a thought, but as something remembered in the body. The way it pressed without forcing. The way it carried without gripping.

She let her breath follow that.

And something shifted.

Time lost its edges after that.

There were no markers for how long it took.

Only movement.

Only breath.

Only the presence of hands that knew where to be before she needed them there.

And then—

The sound.

Not loud.

Not strong.

But unmistakable.

A new voice.

Small, sharp, cutting through everything else.

She opened her eyes.

The aunt was already moving, lifting the child, clearing the first breath, turning it so the body found its place in the world it had just entered.

“It is a girl,” she said.

The words settled into the room without ceremony.

The older woman did not smile.

She nodded once, slow, deliberate.

“Good.”

The child cried again, stronger this time.

The aunt wrapped her in soft cloth, her hands moving quickly, efficiently, then slowed as she brought the child closer.

“Look,” she said.

She did not take the child immediately.

She looked first.

At the face.

At the small movements.

At the way the child’s fingers opened and closed as if already reaching for something unseen.

Then she extended her arms.

The weight of the child settled into her as if it had always been there.

Not heavy.

Not light.

Right.

The crying softened.

The small body adjusted, finding warmth, finding rhythm.

The older woman leaned forward slightly, her eyes moving over both of them.

“She came easily,” she said.

The aunt gave a small sound of agreement.

“She did not fight the path.”

That mattered.

More than the words themselves.

The child shifted again, her face turning slightly against the cloth.

The older woman reached out and touched the child’s forehead with two fingers.

“Then she will move easily between places,” she said.

No one answered.

They did not need to.

The words would be remembered whether spoken again or not.

Outside, the light had begun to change.

The heat was breaking.

A breeze moved through the trees, carrying with it the first hint of evening.

Inside, the fire was fed again—just enough to bring the coals back to life, to restore balance to the room.

The aunt took the child back for a moment, tending to what still needed tending. The older woman helped her shift, easing her body back, arranging skins beneath her so that she could rest without fully leaving the moment.

“You did not break,” the older woman said.

It was not praise.

It was acknowledgment.

She nodded, though her eyes had already begun to close.

When the child was returned to her, the room had grown quieter.

The work had been done.

What remained was holding.

She drew the child close.

The small body fit against her chest, the rhythm of its breathing finding its way into her own.

For a moment—just a moment—everything narrowed to that:

* breath
* warmth
* presence

Nothing else.

Not the river.

Not the village.

Not the paths that would one day carry this child beyond her sight.

Only this.

The older woman watched them both.

“She is not the first,” she said after a time.

She nodded.

“She will not be the last.”

The aunt sat back, wiping her hands on a cloth, her work complete for now.

“She is in the middle,” she added.

The words were simple.

But they held something more.

The older woman’s eyes moved from the child to the mother.

“In the middle,” she repeated.

The child stirred again, her fingers brushing lightly against the cord at her mother’s wrist before settling.

Neither woman spoke.

But both felt it.

Something had been placed.

Not by choice.

Not by decision.

By the simple fact of arrival.

Outside, the first night sounds began to rise.

Inside, the fire steadied.

The child slept.

And somewhere beyond the walls, beyond the trees, beyond the bend of the river, paths that had been walked for generations waited.

Not for this child alone.

But for what she would become within them.

She did not know it.

She did not need to.

The river would carry that forward.

As it always had.
