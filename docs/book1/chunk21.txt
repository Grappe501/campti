The river did not begin where they said it did.

It had no single source that could be pointed to, no place where a person could stand and say, *here is where it starts.* It was already moving when anyone came to know it—already carrying silt from lands unseen, already shaped by bends carved long before memory. The people said it came from far beyond the northern forests, from waters that fed other waters, from rains that fell where no one they knew had walked. But even that was only a way of speaking. The truth of the river was not where it began, but that it **never stopped becoming itself**.

At first light, it moved in silence.

Mist lifted slowly from its surface, not rising all at once but peeling away in thin layers, as though the water were releasing breath it had held through the night. The current did not rush. It pressed forward with a steady force that could be felt more than seen, bending reeds at the edge, slipping past roots that had learned not to resist it. A bird called once from the trees and then again, the second call settling into the rhythm of the morning, as if it had been waiting for the river to answer first.

She stood where the bank softened beneath her feet.

The ground gave slightly as she shifted her weight, cool from the night, holding the shape of where the water had rested before pulling back. She did not look down. She did not need to. The slope, the thickness of the mud, the way the roots curved just beneath the surface—she knew it the way she knew her own breath. There were places along the river where the land resisted, where it held firm and broke the flow into small disruptions. This was not one of them. Here, the earth yielded just enough to belong.

She waited.

Not for something to happen.

For something to settle.

The air still held the last of the night, a quiet that had not yet been broken by movement. Even the insects were slow to begin. There was a moment, just before the day fully entered itself, when everything seemed to hold in place—not stopped, but listening. She felt that moment pass through her, the same way the current moved beneath the surface, unseen but certain.

Behind her, the village had not yet begun to speak.

No fires had been stirred. No voices carried across the open spaces between houses. The world had not asked anything of her yet. That would come. It always did. There were tasks that would be given, paths that would be walked, words that would be spoken at the right time and in the right way. But none of that belonged to this moment.

This moment belonged to the river.

She stepped forward.

Not into it.

Into alignment with it.

Her breath slowed without effort, matching something older than herself, something that did not change even as everything else moved around it. The water curved slightly ahead, disappearing behind a stand of cypress, its direction implied more than seen. She did not follow it with her eyes. She felt where it would go.

This was how she understood the world.

Not as separate things to be named and remembered, but as a continuous movement that could be entered correctly—or not at all. The river did not teach this. It revealed it, the same way it revealed the shape of the land by moving across it. Those who watched long enough began to see. Those who did not were carried without knowing why.

She had been told, when she was younger, that there would come a day when she would step differently into the world. Not as a child who moved through it without being seen, but as someone who would be placed within it, whose movement would begin to shape what came next. The words had been spoken simply, without weight. They had not needed it. The truth of them had already been present, waiting for her to arrive at it.

She did not think of those words now.

She felt them.

In the way the air shifted slightly as the first warmth of the sun reached the tops of the trees.

In the way the bird’s call changed, no longer testing the morning but moving within it.

In the way the river continued, unchanged, as though nothing that would happen this day could alter its path.

She placed her foot where the ground held firm.

And the world, as it had always done, received her exactly as she was.
